HOW IT WORKS

Fund your next trade in 3 steps

From purchase order to payment — trade finance bridges the cash flow gap so you can fulfil orders without draining reserves.

1

Submit your purchase order

Share your purchase order, supplier invoice or letter of credit requirement. We match you with lenders who specialise in your trade corridor.

2

Receive funding

The lender pays your supplier directly (or issues a letter of credit). You receive goods and fulfil your customer’s order without using your own capital.

3

Repay when your customer pays

Once your customer pays for the goods, you repay the trade facility plus fees. The cycle repeats with your next order.

FAQ

Trade finance FAQs

Common questions about trade finance through ElevateLend.

Trade finance is purpose-built for funding specific purchase orders or import shipments. Unlike a business loan (which gives you a lump sum for any purpose), trade finance is tied to a specific transaction. The lender often pays your supplier directly, and you repay when you sell the goods. Terms are shorter (30–180 days vs months or years) and the facility can be used repeatedly for new orders.

Yes. While trade finance is commonly associated with importing and exporting, many providers also fund domestic purchase orders. If you are purchasing goods from an Australian supplier to resell, trade finance (sometimes called purchase order finance) can fund the transaction.

A letter of credit (LC) is a bank-issued guarantee that the supplier will be paid once they fulfil specific conditions (e.g., shipping documents). LCs are common in international trade where buyer and seller do not know each other well. Not all trade finance requires an LC — many transactions use direct supplier payments instead. Your funding specialist will recommend the best structure.

Trade finance facilities typically require appropriate trade credit insurance or marine cargo insurance for international shipments. If goods are damaged or lost, the insurance covers the claim. If goods are simply delayed, the trade facility term may need to be extended — many providers accommodate short extensions. Discuss your risk mitigation options with your funding specialist.
